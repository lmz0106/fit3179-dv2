Stereotypes vs Data Reality — Melbourne, Sydney & Brisbane

We often hear simple weather stereotypes about Australian cities. This project contrasts those beliefs with recent data from the Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) and a second source (Tourism Research Australia or Google Trends), focusing on three complementary lenses: intensity (how much rain), frequency (how often it rains or is sunny), and variability (how stable temperatures are).

Executive summary. Interactive controls expose threshold sensitivity (e.g., what counts as a “rainy day”). A map situates the cities and highlights temperature variability, while a second-source view compares pleasant days with activity/interest indicators. Together, the visuals explain why perceptions can diverge from totals and why robust conclusions need all three lenses.

Rainfall vs. Rainy Days

How to read: Orange = total rainfall (mm) — rain intensity. Blue = number of rainy days — rain frequency. Use the radio buttons (0.2 / 1 / 5 mm) to see how rankings change when the definition of a “rainy day” changes.

Takeaway. The “wettest” city depends on the metric. A city can lead in total rainfall yet not in rainy days, or vice versa. The threshold control lets you explore this sensitivity rather than relying on a single, fixed definition.

Temperature Patterns Across Australia

The map places the three cities for spatial context. Beyond means, what often shapes how weather feels is the spread and stability across the year. (Projection uses Equal Earth for better area fidelity over Australia.)

Why it matters. Two cities with similar means can still feel very different if one shows a wider interquartile range (IQR) or more extremes. Variability often drives comfort and the “four seasons in a day” perception.

Weather and Tourism Correlation

We relate pleasant-weather days (e.g., 20–27 °C and no rain) to a second-source indicator of domestic interest/trips. The dotted trend line summarises the overall direction. Remember: correlation ≠ causation and can be influenced by holidays, events and pricing.

Reading the chart. Each point represents a quarter. If the line slopes upward, quarters with more pleasant days tend to coincide with higher activity — but the strength and stability of this relationship can vary by city and season.

Conclusion

Weather “truths” are multi-dimensional. Rather than asking which city is simply “better,” compare intensity (rainfall in mm), frequency (how many rainy or sunny days) and variability (how wide the temperature range is). With transparent definitions and simple controls, the charts let you test how robust each claim is — beyond the stereotype.

Data & Methods

  • Weather: Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) — Climate Data
  • Tourism: Tourism Research Australia — National Visitor Survey

Definitions. “Rainy day” is user-selectable (0.2 / 1 / 5 mm). “Pleasant day” here is 20–27 °C & no rain (configurable). Variability is emphasised via median and IQR; extremes are noted in tooltips. Weather covers the most recent full year available; the second source uses matched or nearest quarters. Processing: daily → monthly/quarterly aggregates; city-level grouping; missing data flagged or imputed conservatively.

Reproducibility & Performance. Charts are rendered with Vega-Lite and embedded responsively (actions:false, autosize-fit). Data files are kept small for web delivery; map geometry is simplified.

Accessibility: colour-blind-friendly palette (blue for rainfall, orange for rainy days), clear axis units, and plain-text captions summarising each visual.
